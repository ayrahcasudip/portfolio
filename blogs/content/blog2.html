Effective Networking Strategies for Startup Founders

24 May 2024 Business

Networking isn’t just a buzzword—it’s the lifeblood of any successful startup. If you’re a startup founder, you’ve probably heard a thousand times how important networking is. But what does it really mean, and how can you make it work for you?

1. Start with Genuine Connections

Let’s get real: networking isn’t about collecting business cards or LinkedIn connections. It’s about building genuine relationships. When I say “genuine,” I mean it. People can sense when you're only talking to them for your benefit. Instead, approach networking with the mindset of building long-term connections. This means being genuinely interested in others’ stories, challenges, and successes.

Take the example of Ben Silbermann, the co-founder of Pinterest. He didn’t start with a flashy launch or a massive network. He began by reaching out to a small group of bloggers, engaging with them personally, and offering value without expecting anything in return. These relationships helped him build a strong foundation for Pinterest.

2. Leverage the Power of Events

Startup events, pitch competitions, and industry conferences are goldmines for networking. But here’s the thing: just showing up isn’t enough. You need to go in with a game plan.

Think about it this way—before attending an event, research who will be there. Make a list of people you want to connect with and, more importantly, why you want to connect with them. Is it because they’re experts in a field you’re trying to break into? Are they potential partners or mentors?

When you finally meet these people, don’t just talk about your startup. Ask them about their experiences, what challenges they’re facing, and how you might be able to help them. A conversation that starts with “How can I help you?” is much more memorable than a pitch.

3. The Power of the Follow-Up

Here’s a truth bomb: networking doesn’t end when the event does. The follow-up is where the magic happens. If you meet someone interesting, shoot them a quick email or LinkedIn message the next day.

Let me share a quick story: When Melanie Perkins, the CEO of Canva, was in the early stages of her startup, she pitched to hundreds of investors. But it was her persistence in following up, often multiple times, that finally got her a meeting with the right people. That meeting eventually led to Canva securing its first round of funding.

Your follow-up doesn’t have to be lengthy or formal. A simple “It was great meeting you at [Event]. I loved our chat about [Topic]. Let’s keep in touch!” can go a long way.

4. Join (or Create) a Community

One of the best ways to network effectively as a startup founder is by joining communities that resonate with your mission. Whether it’s an online forum, a local meetup group, or an industry-specific Slack channel, being part of a community gives you regular opportunities to connect with like-minded individuals.

If you can’t find a community that fits your needs, create one. For example, David Darmanin, founder of Hotjar, started a simple Slack group for SaaS founders to share insights and support each other. That group grew into a valuable resource, helping members (including Darmanin himself) grow their businesses through shared knowledge and connections.

5. Be a Connector, Not Just a Collector

This is where many people miss the mark. Effective networking isn’t just about who you know—it’s also about who you can connect others with. If you meet someone who could benefit from connecting with someone else in your network, make that introduction.

For example, if you’re talking to a developer and you know a marketer looking for tech support, put them in touch. Not only will both parties appreciate the introduction, but they’ll also remember you as someone who adds value. That’s a powerful reputation to have in the startup world.

6. Consistency is Key

Lastly, consistency is crucial. Networking isn’t a one-off task; it’s an ongoing process. Make it a habit to regularly reach out to your network, attend events, and engage in conversations online and offline.

Take a cue from Reid Hoffman, co-founder of LinkedIn. He’s known for saying, “If you’re not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you’ve launched too late.” The same principle applies to networking—start early, and keep at it, even if it feels awkward at first.

Final Thoughts: Networking is a Two-Way Street

Remember, effective networking is about giving as much as you get. Approach every interaction with a mindset of mutual benefit, and over time, you’ll build a strong, supportive network that can help your startup thrive.

So, what’s your next step? Maybe it’s attending that local startup event, sending that follow-up email, or making an introduction between two people in your network. Whatever it is, start now. Your future self—and your startup—will thank you.
